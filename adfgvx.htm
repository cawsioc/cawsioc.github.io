How it works

The ADFGVX Cipher creates a ciphertext using pairs of letters (digraphs) that encode plaintext characters according to a Polybius-style 6x6 grid whose rows and columns are labelled ADFGVX rather than numerically. Having 36 slots in the grid allows for the full alphabet of 26 letters and the numbers 0 to 9. In this implementation the grid is populated like a Polybius Square by using a "fractionation" keyword. The resultant digraphs are then treated as a stream of single characters and written out under the letters of a second keyword. The final ciphertext is produced by transposing the columns of the digraph stream according to the column ordering produced by rearranging the transposition keyword's letters alphabetically.

For example, the message "Georges Jean Painvin" with the fractionation key "polyb1us" and the transposition key "scytale" would encrypt as follows:

Grid for keyword "p0lyb1us":

U S A C D E
F G H I J K
M N O Q R T
V W X Z 2 3
4 5 6 7 8 9

Processed plaintext message: georgesjeanpainvin

Encryption process:

g is on the "D" column in row "F" and so = DF
e is on the "X" column in row "D" and so = XD
o is on the "F" column in row "G" and so = FG

and so on, producing the digraph stream: DFXDFGVGDFXDDDVFXDFDDGAAFDGFDGAVGFDG

Ciphertext produced by reading columns in keyword alphabetic order: ACELSTY

(col A) FDFDG (col C) FDFAG (col E) VDDFD (col L) GDDGF (col S) DGVGDG (col T) DXDFV (col Y) XFXAA

To recover the original message, the receiver reverses this process. The ADFGVX cipher is not vulnerable to basic frequency analysis techniques but is by no means secure by modern standards.
